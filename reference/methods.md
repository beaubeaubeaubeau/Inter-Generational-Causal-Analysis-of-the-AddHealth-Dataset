## 1. Identification assumptions and target estimand

This is the single most important section for anyone reading coefficient tables or handoff recommendations. What follows is the causal model the screen assumes, the estimand we are targeting, and where the assumption is load-bearing.

> ⚠ **AHPVT serves as the W1 baseline cognitive measure; cognition-outcome estimands are trajectory-adjusted, not level-on-level.** Adjusting for `AH_PVT` (W1 verbal IQ) in a regression of `W4_COG_COMP` on adolescent social integration converts a level-on-level association into an approximate change-from-baseline contrast — i.e. "where you ended up cognitively, *given where you started*." Under the project's research question (cognitive **trajectory** from adolescence into mid-life), this is the right adjustment. The earlier confounder-vs-mediator framing is downgraded: only the strict mediation reading (years-of-pre-W1 social integration → AHPVT → adult cognition) would invalidate it, and that reading requires social experiences before age 12–19 to have causally moved verbal IQ measurably — implausible-but-not-impossible. The trajectory interpretation also rests on three explicit caveats:
>
> 1. **Construct mismatch.** AHPVT measures crystallized vocabulary; `W4_COG_COMP` measures fluid memory (word recall) + working memory (digit span). The two are correlated (literature: r ≈ 0.5–0.7) but not identical. AHPVT is a *proxy* baseline for general cognitive ability, not an identical-construct pre-test. Treat the trajectory β as "trajectory under a vocabulary-anchored baseline."
> 2. **Vocabulary-as-baseline assumes general-g loading.** The interpretation rests on AHPVT being a stable-trait measure of general cognitive ability (g-loaded) rather than a domain-specific verbal skill. If AHPVT picks up specifically verbal experience that is itself shaped by social integration, the proxy leaks.
> 3. **Task 16 front-door** is now a *sensitivity* check, not a load-bearing alternative model. It quantifies how much the trajectory β changes if you assume the strict mediator reading; the *primary* report is the trajectory-adjusted β.

**Target estimand.** The **total effect of W1 social integration on the W4 / W5 outcome's deviation from its baseline-cognition prediction** — i.e. for cognitive outcomes, the trajectory contrast E[Y(x₁) − Y(x₀) | baseline = AHPVT]; for non-cognitive outcomes, the population total effect E[Y(x₁) − Y(x₀)] in the analytic cell. Throughout the project the estimand is **partial / conditional on baseline**, not a population ATE on cognition level.

**Assumed causal structure** (the [DAG library](dag_library.md) renders this graphically per outcome family; the textual sketch is here):

- **Common causes** C flow into both X and Y: demographics (`BIO_SEX`, `RACE`, `PARENT_ED`), W1 mental/health state (`CESD_SUM`, `H1GH1`), and W1 baseline cognition (`AH_PVT`).
- **Assumed adjacencies**: C → X, C → Y, X → Y. No reverse adolescent → adolescent arrow (X is measured at W1, Y at W4 or W5).
- **Assumed absent (unmeasured)**: school climate / context, prenatal & perinatal factors, adolescent diet & physical fitness, family genetic variants. The clean NC battery (Task 16) is what makes this assumption testable; the contaminated `HEIGHT_IN` NC is reported but not load-bearing (see [dataset_manual.md §9](dataset_manual.md#9-outcome-battery-primary--multi-outcome-extension)).
- **Deliberately unadjusted (downstream of X)**: W5 social support (`H5SS*`), W4 biomarker state (CRP, glucose) when outcome is W5. Adjusting for these would block part of the target effect.

**Adjustment-set ladder.** L0 → L0+L1 → L0+L1+AHPVT is designed so the β drift across the three (D4) measures *how much* of the raw signal each tier explains. It is one primary (L0+L1+AHPVT) with two diagnostic reductions.

| Set | Closes back-doors through | Identification role |
|---|---|---|
| L0 | Demographics (sex, race, parental ed) | Minimum acceptable adjustment; most β estimates move substantially when L1 is added if W1 affective state is a confounder |
| L0+L1 | + CES-D + self-rated health at W1 | Blocks confounding via W1 affective / somatic state |
| L0+L1+AHPVT | + W1 verbal IQ as baseline cognition | **Primary spec.** Identifies the trajectory-adjusted effect on cognitive outcomes; for non-cognitive outcomes acts as a general-ability confounder |

**Positivity / overlap.** Required for every stratum of the adjustment set at every level of X. D7 in the diagnostic battery checks this empirically (pass = p̂ ∈ (0.02, 0.98) and eff_N ≥ 500). The screen also has two **structural positivity violations** that are settled by restricting the estimand, *not* by re-weighting:

- **Saturated-school gating** (16 of 24 exposures, all the network-derived ones): centralities (`IDGX2`, `BCENT10X`, etc.) are *structurally undefined* outside saturated schools — there are no peer-roster nominations to compute on. This is positivity = 0, not low overlap. **Decision: do not extrapolate.** The reported estimand for every network exposure is **"ATE within saturated schools"** — full stop. We do not weight up to a counterfactual "if all schools were saturated" estimand, because the saturation-propensity model would be fitting on a structural zero. To make the external-validity gap visible, Task 16 will produce a **saturation-balance table** comparing weighted L0+L1+AHPVT means in saturated vs. non-saturated schools so a reader can judge transportability. Network-exposure plot captions and brief paragraphs must say "within saturated schools" explicitly.
- **Non-network exposures** (8 of 24: `FRIEND_*`, `SCHOOL_BELONG`, `H1FS13`, `H1FS14`, `H1DA7`, `H1PR4`) — sourced from the W1 in-home interview, sample-wide. No saturation restriction; positivity holds.
- **Mode restriction** (W5 cognitive outcomes): only W5 respondents in modes {I, T} received the cognitive battery (~824 of 4,196 once intersected with the W1 in-home sample). (Network ∩ W5 cognitive ≈ 394.) The estimand is the ATE within the mode-eligible subpopulation; generalisability needs IPAW.
- **W4 → W5 attrition.** Differential retention by sex × race. Formal W5-outcome estimation needs **inverse-probability-of-attrition weighting (IPAW)** layered on `GSW5`. Screen uses `GSWGT4_2` uniformly as a comparability shortcut; not estimation-grade.

**What the screen does NOT identify without additional assumptions:**

- Population ATE across non-saturated schools for any network exposure (positivity = 0 by design; the saturation-balance table characterises but does not estimate).
- Direct-only or indirect-only effects through W5 social support (mediator-formula territory; not currently planned).
- Any W6 outcome (Add CAPS cognitive battery is restricted-use; no public-use W6 outcome maps cleanly to the W1 exposure set).

See the [Glossary](glossary.md) for *back-door path*, *positivity*, *negative-control outcome*, *IPAW*; the [DAG library](dag_library.md) for the per-outcome causal graphs; the [experiments index](../experiments/README.md) for the experiment ↔ DAG ↔ method ↔ output mapping.

## 2. Causal-screening diagnostic battery (D1–D9)

Every exposure in the cognitive screen passes through nine diagnostics. Each D tests a specific identification assumption or data-quality condition; passing them together is necessary but not sufficient for a causal interpretation. Full implementation lives in the [cognitive-screening](../experiments/cognitive-screening/) experiment's `run.py`. Per-exposure pass/fail grid: `experiments/cognitive-screening/tables/primary/14_screening_matrix.csv`. Narrative walk-through: [research_journal.md §Phase 4](research_journal.md#phase-4--preliminary-causal-screening-d1d9).

Terms used below (see [Glossary](glossary.md) for plain-language intuition): *back-door path*, *confounder vs. mediator*, *negative-control outcome*, *positivity*, *collider*.

| # | Name | What it checks | Formal pass criterion | Fail means |
|:-:|---|---|---|---|
| **D1** | Baseline significance | Is there any signal at the primary spec (L0+L1+AHPVT) before stability / specificity checks? | p < 0.05 on the exposure coefficient in a WLS fit with `GSWGT4_2` + cluster-robust SE on `CLUSTER2` | No detectable marginal association; later diagnostics are informational only |
| **D2** | Negative-control outcome | Does the exposure predict an outcome it *shouldn't* (`HEIGHT_IN`)? If yes, unmeasured confounding is likely. | p ≥ 0.10 on exposure → adult height, under the primary adjustment set | NC-level confounding is detected — but see the contamination caveat in [dataset_manual.md §9](dataset_manual.md#9-outcome-battery-primary--multi-outcome-extension) |
| **D3** | Sibling dissociation | Is the target effect larger than a "related but weaker" sibling exposure, and in the same sign? Same-school / same-grid contrast. | `sign(β_target) == sign(β_sibling)` **AND** `\|β_target\| > \|β_sibling\|` **AND** `\|β_target − β_sibling\| > sqrt(se_t² + se_s²)` | Target and sibling are indistinguishable → target probably isn't capturing exposure-specific signal. **D3 is N/A for network exposures** (decision 2026-04-26 / TODO §A16): the within-network-file siblings — `ODGX2` for `IDGX2`/`BCENT10X` etc. — are too construct-correlated to dissociate, and no structurally-independent placebo is available within the network data. D2 and D7 carry the confounding-detection load for these exposures. |
| **D4a** | Adjustment-set stability — *sensitivity* | How much does β drift L0 → L0+L1 (adding W1 health/affect)? This is the real adjustment-set sensitivity check: a big drift here = hidden confounding via W1 covariates. | Relative shift `(\|β_L0 − β_L0+L1\|) / \|β_L0+L1\| < 0.30` **AND** sign stable | β depends on whether W1 health/affect is in the adjustment set — confounding via the L1 covariate set |
| **D4b** | Estimand presentation — *L0+L1 vs L0+L1+AHPVT*, no pass/fail | Side-by-side reporting of two distinct estimands: `β_levels` (L0+L1 spec, "level estimand") vs `β_trajectory` (L0+L1+AHPVT spec, "trajectory estimand from baseline cognition"). Per the trajectory framing decision ([§1](#1-identification-assumptions-and-target-estimand)), AHPVT is a baseline cognitive measure, NOT a covariate to "robustness-check" the L0+L1 β against. The shift between the two specs is *expected* — for cognitive outcomes, AHPVT is by design eating the level-of-cognition signal so that the trajectory-of-cognition signal can show through. | No pass/fail. Reports `β_levels`, `β_trajectory`, absolute shift, and pct-attenuation. | Informative for interpretation only. Big AHPVT-driven attenuation on cognitive outcomes is the headline trajectory finding, not a confounding signal. (Reframed 2026-04-26 / TODO §A18 — the prior single-D4 metric mathematically inflated when β_full → 0, making cognitive exposures "fail" by tautology.) |
| **D5** | Outcome-component consistency | Do the three W4 cognitive sub-tests (immediate/delayed word recall, digit span) line up in sign? Cognition only. | All three component β in the same direction as the composite **AND ≥ 2 of 3 with p < 0.10**, no opposite-sign dissent | Composite signal is driven by one sub-test; spurious or narrow construct |
| **D6** | Dose-response monotonicity | For a continuous exposure, does the outcome move monotonically across exposure quintiles? | `|Spearman ρ| > 0.6` across quintile means **AND** `monotone_sign` holds (no mid-range inversion); the signed Spearman's absolute value captures monotonicity in either direction. Threshold is the conventional dose-response cutoff (loosened from 0.7 on 2026-04-26). | Non-monotonic response — linear-in-X spec is mis-specified |
| **D7** | Positivity / overlap | Is there non-zero probability of every exposure level at every covariate stratum? Q5-vs-Q1 logit (continuous) or binary balance (binary). | Fitted p̂ ∈ (0.02, 0.98) AND effective N ≥ 500 after overlap trimming | No usable comparison group in parts of covariate space; estimate is extrapolation |
| **D8** | Saturated-school selection penalty | Informational. How much of W1 is lost to the network-file saturation gate for this specific exposure? | Reports N(analytic) / N(W1) ratio; no pass/fail threshold | External-validity caveat — estimand is within-saturated-schools |
| **D9** | Collider / double-adjustment red flags | Hard-coded list of known bad-combination exposures (e.g. using `H1FS13` as exposure while adjusting for `CESD_SUM`, which contains `H1FS13`). | Not on the red-flag list | Collider bias or mechanical double-counting; drop from shortlist |

**Composite score.** Each exposure is scored 0–8 based on passes across D1–D9 (D8 is informational; red-flags discount the score). Categories: **Strong** (score ≥ 7), **Mixed** (5–6), **Weakened** (3–4), **Drop** (< 3 or D9 red flag). The task-16 handoff is drawn from Strong + Mixed, never from Drop.

**Why the battery is not sufficient.** Passing D1–D9 does *not* prove a causal effect — it only rules out nine specific failure modes. Unmeasured confounders outside the L0+L1+AHPVT set (school climate, early-childhood cognition, family SES beyond parental education) are still possible. Task 16's job is to bound those via front-door decomposition, E-value-style sensitivity, and a cleaner NC battery.

## 3. Estimator details

This section describes the estimators invoked by the screening and handoff experiments. Each subsection is self-contained: what the method does, when it applies in this project, a short reference for further reading. Method names elsewhere in the repo (in experiment `report.md` files, in figure prose) link here on first use.

### Weighted least squares (WLS) with cluster-robust SE

Weighted least squares fits a linear regression in which each observation contributes proportionally to its survey weight, and standard errors are computed treating each primary sampling unit (PSU) — here `CLUSTER2`, the high-school / feeder-middle-school pair, of which there are 132 — as a single independent unit for variance accounting. The point estimate β̂ minimises Σwᵢ(yᵢ − xᵢβ)², and the variance estimator is the Liang–Zeger sandwich form Vᶜˡᵘˢ = (X′WX)⁻¹ (Σ_g X_g′W_g e_g e_g′W_g X_g) (X′WX)⁻¹ summed over the H = 132 clusters. The implementation defers to statsmodels' built-in cluster-robust covariance with `use_correction=True` (which applies the small-sample factor `H/(H−1) · (n−1)/(n−k)`) and `use_t=True`; degrees of freedom for t-stats and CIs are statsmodels' default `n − k` (decision 2026-04-26: trust the package's correction rather than mixing two correction philosophies via a manual H−1 override). Survey weights are normalised to mean 1 before being passed to the WLS fit so that `use_correction`'s implicit assumption `E[w·e²] = σ²` operates on the right scale; the point estimate is invariant to weight rescaling. Cluster-robust SE is necessary because Add Health is school-clustered: ignoring the clustering shrinks SEs by the design effect and inflates type-I error. WLS + cluster-robust SE on `CLUSTER2` is the workhorse estimator across the entire screening pipeline — used in [cognitive-screening](../experiments/cognitive-screening/), [multi-outcome-screening](../experiments/multi-outcome-screening/), and as the base estimator in the four planned handoff experiments ([cardiometabolic-handoff](../experiments/cardiometabolic-handoff/), [ses-handoff](../experiments/ses-handoff/), [cognitive-frontdoor](../experiments/cognitive-frontdoor/), [negative-control-battery](../experiments/negative-control-battery/)). Reference: Wooldridge (2010), *Econometric Analysis of Cross Section and Panel Data*, 2nd ed., MIT Press, Ch. 20 (cluster-sample methods).

### Inverse-probability-of-attrition weighting (IPAW)

Inverse-probability-of-attrition weighting reweights respondents who remained in a wave to also represent the comparable respondents who dropped out, under the assumption that attrition is independent of the outcome conditional on observed pre-attrition covariates (missing-at-random). In practice we fit a logistic regression of "remained at wave Wₖ" on a rich pre-attrition covariate set (demographics, prior-wave cognition, socioeconomic status, prior-wave health), compute the predicted retention probability p̂ᵢ for each respondent who remained, and reweight by the *stabilised* multiplier `P(retain) / p̂ᵢ` (Cole & Hernán 2008) — typically multiplied into the existing post-stratification weight (here `GSW5`) and then trimmed at the 95th percentile to bound influence. Stabilisation yields a lower-variance estimator with the same expected value as the un-stabilised `1 / p̂ᵢ` form. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/ipw.py:fit_ipaw` (TDD-tested, 5 tests pass: identity-when-no-attrition, stabilisation form, trim threshold, recovery-of-known-bias, output-aligned). First used in [ses-handoff](../experiments/ses-handoff/) (2026-04-27). The screening pipeline still uses `GSWGT4_2` uniformly as a comparability shortcut (not estimation-grade). Reference: Cole & Hernán (2008), "Constructing inverse probability weights for marginal structural models," *American Journal of Epidemiology* 168(6): 656-664; Hernán & Robins (2020), *Causal Inference: What If*, Ch. 12, Chapman & Hall.

### Front-door decomposition (three-equation)

The front-door criterion identifies the X → Y causal effect through a fully mediating variable M when back-door adjustment fails (typically because of unmeasured X–Y confounding). It requires three structural conditions: (i) X → M → Y with no direct X → Y arrow, (ii) all of the X → Y effect passes through M, and (iii) no unmeasured M–Y confounders. Operationally we estimate three regressions: M on X (with adjustment for the front-door confounder set), Y on M and X (adjusting for the M–Y confounder set), and combine them via Pearl's front-door formula E[Y | do(X = x)] = Σ_m P(M = m | X = x) · Σ_{x'} E[Y | M = m, X = x'] P(X = x'). In this project the front-door is a *sensitivity check*, not a primary identification: [cognitive-frontdoor](../experiments/cognitive-frontdoor/) (executed 2026-04-27) quantifies how much the trajectory-adjusted social-integration → cognition β changes if one assumes the strict mediator reading of AHPVT (i.e. social integration → AHPVT → W4 cognition). **Headline finding**: the trajectory β shrinks ~70% across all 3 shortlisted exposures (IDGX2 −74%, ODGX2 −69%, BCENT10X −73%) under the strict-mediator reading. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/frontdoor.py:frontdoor_decompose` (3 tests pass: indirect-effect recovery, contract, constant-mediator-zero). Reference: Pearl (2009), *Causality*, 2nd ed., Cambridge University Press, §3.3.2 (the front-door criterion).

### E-values

The E-value is VanderWeele & Ding's bound on how strong an unmeasured confounder would have to be — both in its association with the exposure and in its association with the outcome — to fully explain away an observed effect estimate. For a risk ratio RR > 1, E-value = RR + sqrt(RR · (RR − 1)); for continuous outcomes, the estimate is first converted to an approximate RR via a standardised-mean-difference transform. A small E-value (close to 1) means a weak unmeasured confounder could explain the observation away — the result is fragile. A large E-value means even very strong unmeasured confounding could not eliminate the result — the result is robust. In this project E-values are reported in every handoff experiment alongside the point estimate and confidence interval, giving the reader a single number for "how much hidden confounding does this claim survive?" Implementation: `scripts/analysis/sensitivity.py:evalue`. Reference: VanderWeele & Ding (2017), "Sensitivity Analysis in Observational Research: Introducing the E-Value," *Annals of Internal Medicine* 167(4): 268–274.

### Cornfield bound (bias factor B)

The Cornfield bound formalises how strong an unmeasured confounder U must be to fully explain away an observed exposure-outcome association. For binary exposure A and outcome Y with unmeasured confounder U, the bias factor under the null "all observed association is confounding" is `B = (RR_AU · RR_UY) / (RR_AU + RR_UY − 1)`, where `RR_AU` is the relative risk of U=1 across A-groups and `RR_UY` is the relative risk of Y=1 across U-groups (Theorem 2.1 of Ch. 6 of the user's MA 592 textbook). The observed RR is bounded by `RR_obs ≤ RR_true · B`, so we can solve for the (RR_AU, RR_UY) pair that would just explain away an observed RR. Whereas the E-value reports a single robustness number, the Cornfield bound traces the full robustness curve in (RR_AU, RR_UY) space. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/sensitivity.py:cornfield_bound`. In this project Cornfield is reported alongside E-value in handoff experiments to give the reader both a single-number summary and a curve.

### η-tilt sensitivity (general-ATE bound)

The η-tilt extends sensitivity bounding from binary outcomes to general ATEs (Ch. 6 §3 of the user's textbook). Define `η_a(X) = E[Y(a) | A = a, X] / E[Y(a) | A = 1−a, X]` — the ratio of the potential-outcome mean for treatment-arm a between observed-treated and observed-untreated subgroups, conditional on covariates X. Under conditional exchangeability `η_1 = η_0 = 1` and the formula collapses to plain ATE = E[μ_1(X) − μ_0(X)] (Remark 6 of Ch. 6). For user-specified `η_1, η_0 ≠ 1`, the formula returns the perturbed ATE estimate; sweeping over `(η_1, η_0)` traces an ATE-bound surface in sensitivity-parameter space. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/sensitivity.py:eta_tilt`. In this project η-tilt is the primary sensitivity tool for continuous outcomes (BMI, cognition composite, earnings brackets), where E-value's RR-based formula is awkward.

### Bias-corrected matching (Abadie–Imbens 2006, Prop. 5.1)

M-nearest-neighbour matching estimates ATE by imputing each unit's missing counterfactual as the mean of its M nearest opposite-treatment neighbours, then averaging the unit-level treatment-effect estimates. The plain matching estimator suffers from bias when the matched neighbours are not exact matches on continuous covariates — the residual covariate gap leaks into the imputed counterfactual. The bias-corrected version (Prop. 5.1 of Ch. 5 of the user's MA 592 textbook) plugs in a regression-helper estimate of the conditional outcome surface, AIPW-style: `ATE = (1/n) Σ_i [μ̂_1(X_i) − μ̂_0(X_i) + I(A=1)(1+KM_i/M)(Y_i − μ̂_1(X_i)) − I(A=0)(1+KM_i/M)(Y_i − μ̂_0(X_i))]`, where `KM_i` is the *used-as-a-match* count for unit i (Ch. 5 gotcha: this is *not* "i's own matches"). In this project the bias-corrected matching estimator is the project's first use of matching, in `experiments/em-compensatory-by-ses/` for the binary subgroup contrast (low-SES top-quintile-IDGX2 vs. low-SES bottom-quintile-IDGX2). Variance: Abadie–Imbens analytic formula via the conditional variance estimate `σ̂_i² = (J/(J+1)) · within-cluster Σ(Y_j − ȳ)²` over each unit's J same-treatment neighbours; bootstrap is **invalid** for fixed-M matching (Ch. 5 §9). Implementation: `scripts/analysis/matching.py`. Mahalanobis distance is used by default for the M-NN step (scale-invariant; see Ch. 5 §4). Reference: Abadie & Imbens (2006), "Large Sample Properties of Matching Estimators for Average Treatment Effects," *Econometrica* 74(1): 235–267.

### Doubly-robust AIPW (Robins–Rotnitzky–Zhao)

The augmented inverse-probability-weighted (AIPW) estimator combines a propensity-score model and an outcome-regression model into a single ATE estimator that is consistent if **either** nuisance is correctly specified — hence "doubly robust." For each unit i with binary treatment A_i, propensity p̂_i = P(A_i = 1 | X_i), and outcome predictions μ̂_1(X_i), μ̂_0(X_i), the influence function is `φ_i = μ̂_1(X_i) − μ̂_0(X_i) + (A_i / p̂_i)(Y_i − μ̂_1(X_i)) − ((1−A_i)/(1−p̂_i))(Y_i − μ̂_0(X_i))` and the ATE is `mean(φ)` with sandwich variance `var(φ) / n`. Cross-fitting (sample-splitting) keeps nuisance estimation honest: within each fold, train both nuisances on the *other* folds and predict on the held-out fold. In this project DR-AIPW is the doubly-robust sensitivity check inside the handoff experiments alongside the WLS primary estimate. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/dr_aipw.py` (default propensity model is `LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)`, default outcome model is `LinearRegression`; both override-able). Reference: Robins, Rotnitzky & Zhao (1994), "Estimation of Regression Coefficients When Some Regressors Are Not Always Observed," *Journal of the American Statistical Association* 89(427): 846–866.

### Placebo permutation

A placebo permutation test refits the primary specification many times after randomly permuting the exposure across observations (or across PSUs, to preserve cluster structure), generating a null distribution of β̂ under the sharp null of zero exposure effect. The empirical p-value is the fraction of permutations producing a |β̂| as extreme as the observed estimate. Unlike the parametric p-value, the permutation p does not rely on the residuals being approximately Gaussian or the SE estimator being correctly specified — it answers "could chance allocation of exposure labels produce this estimate?" In this project placebo permutation is part of the cognitive-screening sensitivity battery (`tables/sensitivity/11_placebo_permutation.csv`); it is also planned as a robustness check inside each handoff experiment. We typically run 1,000 permutations within each respondent's cluster to preserve the design. Reference: Imbens & Rubin (2015), *Causal Inference for Statistics, Social, and Biomedical Sciences*, Cambridge University Press, Ch. 5 (Fisherian randomisation inference).

### Balance tables

A balance table is a side-by-side comparison of covariate means (and standardised mean differences) between two groups — typically treated vs. control, or in our case saturated- vs. non-saturated-school respondents. Continuous covariates report weighted means and SDs in each group plus the standardised mean difference (SMD = |x̄₁ − x̄₂| / pooled SD); binary covariates report proportions and the absolute proportion difference. SMDs above ~0.10 conventionally flag meaningful imbalance. Balance tables are descriptive, not inferential — they do not test a hypothesis, they show the reader where the comparison groups differ on observables. In this project balance tables appear in two places: (i) the [cognitive-screening](../experiments/cognitive-screening/) verification battery (`tables/verification/13_attrition_ipw.csv` for W4→W5 dropouts vs. retainers, `tables/verification/13_negctrl_*` for negative-control checks); (ii) the planned [saturation-balance](../experiments/saturation-balance/) experiment, which characterises the external-validity gap between saturated and non-saturated schools so a reader can judge transportability of the network-exposure ATE. Reference: Stuart (2010), "Matching Methods for Causal Inference: A Review and a Look Forward," *Statistical Science* 25(1): 1–21, §4.2 (covariate balance diagnostics).

### Ordered logit and interval regression

**Ordered logit** is the standard regression for ordinal outcomes — categories with meaningful order but no metric distance, e.g. a 5-level "low / med-low / med / med-high / high" obesity-class scale. It models the cumulative probability P(Y ≤ k | X) = logit⁻¹(α_k − Xβ) under the proportional-odds assumption: a one-unit shift in X has the same coefficient β across every category boundary k. The proportional-odds assumption is testable (Brant test) and not always satisfied; if it fails, one falls back to a generalised ordered logit or multinomial logit. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/ordered_logit.py:fit_ordered_logit` wraps `statsmodels.miscmodels.OrderedModel` with cluster-robust SE via the score-sandwich form; mean-1 weight rescaling per the project convention (4 tests pass).

**Interval regression** handles outcomes observed only as bracketed intervals (e.g. self-reported earnings reported in bands like "$15,000–$24,999"). It treats the latent value as continuous within each bracket and estimates the model by maximising a likelihood that integrates the Gaussian density over each respondent's reported bracket — equivalent to a Tobit-like censored-likelihood approach with two-sided censoring per observation. Implementation: `scripts/analysis/interval_regression.py:fit_interval` uses direct `scipy.optimize.minimize` on the censored Gaussian negative log-likelihood (no new dependency); cluster-robust SE via score-sandwich (4 tests pass). **Numerical caveat**: the BFGS optimisation is fragile when σ² is on a wildly different scale from β (e.g. raw-dollar earnings → σ² ~ 10¹⁰); pre-rescale bracket bounds to keep β·σ near unit scale before calling.

In this project: ordered logit applies to `H4BMICLS` (1–6 adult BMI class) inside [cardiometabolic-handoff](../experiments/cardiometabolic-handoff/) (executed 2026-04-27, β = −0.045 log-odds, E-value 1.49). Interval regression applies to `H5EC1` (1–13 bracketed adult earnings, brackets stored in thousands of dollars to avoid the σ-scale fragility) inside [ses-handoff](../experiments/ses-handoff/) (executed 2026-04-27, β = +1.09 k$/ODGX2-unit, E-value 1.28). Reference: Long & Freese (2014), *Regression Models for Categorical Dependent Variables Using Stata*, 3rd ed., Stata Press, Ch. 7 (ordinal outcomes) and Ch. 8 (censored and interval-coded outcomes).
